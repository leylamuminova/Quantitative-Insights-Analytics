Introduction

Healthcare costs vary not only by smoking habits but also by other personal characteristics such as age, body mass index (BMI), and region of residence. Older individuals, for instance, generally face higher medical expenses, while higher BMI values are linked to obesity-related conditions. Regional differences may also reflect variations in healthcare prices and lifestyle patterns.

The goal of this project is to build a multivariable regression model to explain how demographic and lifestyle factors - specifically age, BMI, number of children, smoker status, sex, and region - affect medical insurance charges. By including several predictors at once, we can see not only whether each variable matters, but also whether its effect remains important after accounting for the others.

In my first project I examined only smoking status and found that smokers paid much higher charges. In this second project I extend that analysis by including additional predictors such as age, BMI, number of children, sex, and region. This allows me to see whether smoking remains an important factor even after adjusting for other influences

Data

The dataset comes from the Medical Insurance Cost Dataset available on Kaggle.
It contains information for individuals in the United States, including their age, gender, BMI, number of children, smoking status, region, and annual insurance charges.

For this project I used a subset of roughly 130 observations from the full dataset -enough to fit a reliable multiple regression model while keeping the analysis readable.

Variable Type Description
age Quantitative Age of the individual (in years)
sex Categorical Biological sex (male, female)
bmi Quantitative Body Mass Index
children Quantitative Number of children covered
smoker Categorical Smoking status (yes, no)
region Categorical Residential region (northeast, northwest, southeast, southwest)
charges Quantitative Yearly insurance charges (USD)

The response variable is charges, and the explanatory variables are the remaining six columns. This dataset is appropriate for a multiple regression model because it includes several quantitative and categorical predictors, a meaningful numerical outcome, and realistic variation among observations.

Main Explanatory Variable

For this project, I chose smoker as my main explanatory variable because smoking is one of the strongest behavioral factors affecting health and medical costs. Smoking increases the risk of chronic diseases and therefore leads to higher insurance charges. However, other factors like age, BMI, and region might also play a role. This project will show whether the relationship between smoking and charges remains strong even after accounting for these other influences.

Exploring the Relationship Between Smoker and Charges

To visualize the relationship between smoking status and insurance charges, I created a side-by-side boxplot.

The boxplot shows a strong difference between the two groups. Smokers have much higher insurance charges overall—their median charge is above the upper quartile for non-smokers. The entire box for smokers is shifted upward, meaning that nearly all smokers pay more than most non-smokers.

Overall, the plot clearly suggests that smoking is strongly associated with higher medical insurance charges, making it a sensible main explanatory variable for further modeling.

Checking for Multicollinearity

Multicollinearity occurs when two or more explanatory variables are highly correlated, meaning one can almost be expressed as a combination of the others. In such cases, the model has difficulty separating their individual effects, and the estimated coefficients can become unstable or hard to interpret.

To check for this problem in my dataset, I examined the relationships among the quantitative predictors: age, BMI, and number of children.

# Examine pairwise correlations
num <- with(insurance, data.frame(age, bmi, children))
round(cor(num), 3)
           age   bmi children
age      1.000 0.109    0.042

All correlation values are well below 0.4, showing that none of these variables are highly related or redundant. A scatterplot matrix confirmed that there is no clear linear pattern among them - variable provides distinct information about the individual.

Because none of the predictors are strongly correlated, my model does not suffer from multicollinearity. This means it is reasonable to interpret the effects of each variable while holding the others constant. Unlike the CPS85 example we studied in class, where exper could almost be calculated from age and educ, no variable in this dataset can be expressed as a near combination of the others. Therefore, all predictors were retained in the final model.

Overall Summary and Conclusion

The purpose of this project was to build a multivariable regression model to explain variation in medical insurance charges using both demographic and lifestyle variables. I began with a simple model that included only smoking status and found that it explained about 62 % of the variation in charges. After gradually adding age, BMI, number of children, sex, and region, the model’s fit improved substantially, and the final model - charges ~ smoker * bmi + age + children + sex + region - achieved an R² of 0.84.

The results make strong practical sense. Smoking remains the most important predictor, followed by age and BMI. The significant interaction between smoking and BMI shows that heavier smokers face especially high medical costs, which reflects real health risk patterns. Children also have a small positive effect, while sex and region contribute relatively little once other variables are considered.

Model diagnostics showed that residuals were reasonably well-behaved, and the correlation and scatterplot analysis confirmed that there was no evidence of multicollinearity. The model is therefore statistically sound and interpretable.

Overall, this final model is satisfactory because it explains a large portion of the variation in charges while remaining simple enough to interpret. Adding more variables might increase R² slightly, but it would not meaningfully improve understanding. The project demonstrates that demographic and lifestyle factors - especially smoking and BMI - are powerful predictors of medical insurance costs.
